Final Verdict

The uncomfortable
answer

When Loans Work
Stable institutions and governance
Local suppliers can absorb contract value
People-based sectors — education, public admin
Country has capacity to execute projects
Examples: Morocco, Bangladesh, Armenia
When Loans Fail
Conflict or political instability
Foreign suppliers capture majority of value
Specialized sectors — energy, ICT, health
Weak institutions can't execute projects
Examples: Benin, Cameroon, Timor-Leste, Myanmar

World Bank loans are not inherently good or bad for developing countries. They are a tool. In stable hands with strong institutions, they accelerate development. In fragile states with weak governance, they create debt without development.

The cruelest irony is that the countries most targeted for loans — the poorest and most fragile — are precisely the ones least equipped to benefit from them. They lose the most contract money to foreign suppliers, cancel the most projects, absorb funds the least efficiently, and show the weakest correlation between borrowing and outcomes.

The World Bank is not the villain. But the system consistently delivers better results for countries that need it least, and worse results for countries that need it most. That is not a coincidence. That is a structural problem.
