# Final Conclusion — Are World Bank Loans Worth It?

## The Question
> Are World Bank loans actually worth it for developing countries?

This analysis examined 273,497 contracts, 11,236 loans, 211 countries,
and 1,500 education data points across 3 layers of investigation.
Here is what the data says.

---

## Layer 1 — The Money Trail: Who Actually Gets the Money?

**The headline finding:**
On the surface, 55% of contract value stays local. But this average
hides a deeply unequal reality.

**The real story:**
- Low income countries lose 53.57% of contract value to foreign suppliers
  — the only income group where foreigners capture the majority
- High income countries keep 77% locally
- The poorer the country, the more the money leaves immediately

**China is the single biggest winner:**
China captured 21 billion USD in World Bank contracts — nearly double
the second place country. It dominates as a supplier across every
income group except high income. Low income countries borrowing from
the World Bank are effectively funding Chinese infrastructure companies.

**The worst sectors:**
Energy and Extractives bleeds 71% to foreign suppliers. When a poor
country borrows for power infrastructure, 71 cents of every dollar
leaves immediately. Education is the rare exception at only 23% foreign.

**Layer 1 verdict:**
> A low income country borrowing for an energy project is taking on
> debt to pay foreign companies — while its own economy sees less
> than half of the money it borrowed.

---

## Layer 2 — The Loan Itself: Are Countries Drowning in Debt?

**The headline finding:**
88% of all World Bank loan value — 548 billion USD — is still active
and outstanding. The full repayment burden has not yet arrived.

**The real story:**
- Only 75.5% of promised money actually reaches borrowing countries
- Low income countries absorb only 72.5% — the worst of any group
- Upper middle income countries absorb 91.5% — the most efficiently

**Cancellations hit the most fragile:**
Timor-Leste cancelled 51.57% of all its loans. Myanmar 40.80%.
Zimbabwe 27.65%. The countries that needed loans most were least
able to use them — because conflict, instability, and weak
institutions killed projects before they could deliver.

**The debt trap is not where you expect:**
Lower middle income countries carry the heaviest debt burden at
57.70% of disbursed amounts still owed — not the poorest countries.
IDA soft loans protect the very poorest, but the middle tier —
Nigeria, Pakistan, Bangladesh, Tanzania — is accumulating debt
that will consume future budgets for decades.

**The Morocco exception:**
Morocco borrowed from IDA when poor, repaid everything, and
graduated to IBRD as it developed. This proves loans can work —
but only when a country has stable institutions and governance
to execute projects and generate the growth needed to repay.

**Layer 2 verdict:**
> The loan is not the problem. But 45 billion USD was cancelled,
> 106 billion USD sits undisbursed, and 548 billion USD is still
> owed. The system around the loan — whether projects get executed,
> whether institutions are strong enough — determines whether
> borrowing was worth it.

---

## Layer 3 — The Impact: Did Loans Change Anything on the Ground?

**The headline finding:**
There is virtually no correlation between how much a country borrowed
and whether its education outcomes improved.

**The real story:**
- Primary enrollment is already near universal — loans may have
  helped decades ago but have nothing left to fix
- Secondary enrollment improved 8.5 points from 2016 to 2020
  before COVID disrupted progress
- The correlation between total loans and education improvement
  is 0.10 — essentially zero

**The damning comparison:**
- Benin: 7.4 billion USD, 204 loans → enrollment dropped 27 points
- Burkina Faso: 10.3 billion USD, 230 loans → dropped 5.9 points
- Angola: 1 billion USD, 24 loans → improved 15.7 points
- Costa Rica: 6.4 million USD, 1 loan → improved 3.3 points

More money does not produce better outcomes. The real drivers are
political stability, governance quality, and the absence of conflict.
None of which a loan can directly buy.

**Layer 3 verdict:**
> Pouring money into a country at war or with broken institutions
> does not build schools. It builds debt.

---

## The Final Answer

**Are World Bank loans worth it for developing countries?**

### When loans work:
- The country has stable institutions and governance
- Local suppliers can absorb the contract value
- The sector is people-based — education, public admin
- The country has the capacity to execute projects fully
- Morocco, Bangladesh, Armenia are examples of this path

### When loans do not work:
- The country is in conflict or political instability
- Foreign suppliers capture the majority of contract value
- The sector requires specialized technology — energy, ICT, health
- The country lacks capacity to absorb and execute
- Benin, Cameroon, Timor-Leste, Myanmar show this path

### The uncomfortable truth:
> World Bank loans are not inherently good or bad for developing
> countries. They are a tool. In stable hands with strong institutions,
> they accelerate development. In fragile states with weak governance,
> they create debt without development.
>
> The cruelest irony is that the countries most targeted for loans
> — the poorest and most fragile — are precisely the ones least
> equipped to benefit from them. They lose the most contract money
> to foreign suppliers, cancel the most projects, absorb funds the
> least efficiently, and show the weakest correlation between
> borrowing and outcomes.
>
> The World Bank is not evil. But the system it operates in
> consistently delivers better results for countries that need
> it least and worse results for countries that need it most.
> That is not a coincidence. That is a structural problem.

---

## Data Limitations
- Contracts data covers 2019-2026 only — loans go back to 1961
- Education data covers only 26 countries in the final merged dataset
- Correlation does not imply causation — many external factors
  affect education outcomes beyond World Bank loans
- Morocco and many other countries are absent from education data
  due to API limitations in the original data pull

---

## What This Analysis Does Not Answer
- Long term impact of loans beyond the 2015-2025 window
- Quality of education, not just enrollment rates
- Impact of loans on GDP, poverty rates, or health outcomes
- Whether alternative financing mechanisms would perform better
- The role of loan conditionalities in project success or failure
